01 — The Problem

Students lose 45 minutes a day to inbox noise

University students live in an institutional communication jungle. Class announcements, event invitations, administrative notices, academic deadlines, and personal emails all land in the same inbox. There's no signal-to-noise filter. Students either read everything (inefficient) or nothing (risky). Both paths create anxiety.

02 — Discovery Process

How I validated the problem

I started with casual observation. Watched LUMS students check their email. Asked questions. Most said they felt "behind" on important announcements. Specific email quote: "I miss deadlines because everything feels equally important."

Then ran 30+ semi-structured interviews across three student populations:

  • CS/Engineering students (n=12): Comfortable with complexity. Want granular control.
  • Business/Social Sciences (n=11): Overwhelmed by settings. Prefer simplicity.
  • Humanities/Languages (n=8): Heavy email users but rarely check settings.

Conducted a 47-question survey asking about pain points, workarounds, and acceptable solutions. Key finding: not a volume problem (most get 20–30 emails daily). It's a triage problem. No one has time to read email metadata or remember sender priorities.

Competitive Audit

Reviewed 8 email clients (Gmail, Outlook, Spark, Hey, Newton, Superhuman, Mimestream, Shortwave). All treated email as a volume management problem, not a triage problem. None understood the specific context of university communication.

03 — Core Insight

The emotional angle matters more than the feature angle

When I started the project, I thought the problem was "how do we reduce email volume?" But interviews showed the real problem was anxiety. Students described their inboxes as "stressful," "overwhelming," and "daunting." One student said: "I have an Outlook tab open but never actually look at it. I just check it when I'm desperate."

The feature set needed to reduce not just time spent on email, but the emotional burden of knowing what you're missing.

04 — Design Iterations

Three alternatives tested in paper prototypes

Alternative A: Smart Sorting + AI Tuning

Cards grouped by category (Classes, Events, Announcements, Personal). AI learns from what users open and re-sorts. Tapping a card marks it as "read." Users could refine preferences in a Settings tab.

Alternative B: Ambient Awareness Feed

A passive feed showing only "important but non-urgent" updates. Think: a read-only Bulletin Board for events and deadlines. Separate from the main inbox. The thinking: if you don't have to actively triage, anxiety drops.

Alternative C: Gestures + Quick Actions

Swipe left to purge. Swipe right to pin. Tap to open. No settings menu — just actions. Inspired by mobile messaging apps. Fast, tactile, low-friction.

Paper testing revealed: Alt A's card layout worked. Alt C's gestures resonated with mobile-first students. Alt B's "ambient awareness" concept was right, but implementation was too dense — felt like a second inbox.

The final design merged all three. Smart sorting + gestures + a redesigned Discover tab with separated Daily Digest (text) and Bulletin Board (events).

05 — Final Feature Set

Four features that defend against inbox anxiety

🔕

Frictionless Onboarding

Login with Outlook. Mute your top 3 bad senders in one screen. The AI learns and re-sorts from day one. No settings pages. No configuration tax.

📬

Today Inbox

Only today's emails. Auto-purged at midnight. Card layout shows subject, sender, preview. Swipe left to purge, right to pin. Fast triage.

📅

Intelligent Calendar

Pulls class schedules from Zambeel and due dates from the LMS. Detects dates inside emails. One-tap "Add to Calendar" buttons inline.

🗺️

Ambient Awareness

Daily Digest (1–3 sentences of urgent items) + Bulletin Board (societies post event posters). Discovery without inbox noise.

06 — Visual Language

Why Neo-Brutalism for a student inbox

Most university software was designed in 2014 and hasn't been touched. Safe sans-serifs, rounded cards, blue-and-white color schemes. It communicates institutional blandness better than information.

I went the other direction: Neo-Brutalism. Heavy 2–3px solid borders. Hard offset shadows. Sharp corners. No rounding softening everything. It creates hierarchy through weight and contrast rather than gradients. On a phone screen with fast-moving cards, that reads clearly.

There was also a brand logic. The target users are university students — a demographic with a finely-tuned sensitivity to anything that feels corporate or automated. The visual identity needed to feel made, not generated. The palette anchors in LUMS institutional color (indigo) + the gold accent from the SBASSE building.

07 — Lessons
1
The interesting finding usually comes from interview #8, not #2.

Early interviews pointed toward a volume problem. Deeper into the phase, the emotional angle surfaced: dread, anxiety. Mixed-methods research earns its name.

2
Paper prototypes catch obvious failures fast.

We almost shipped "Ambient Awareness" as a dense feed. Testing on paper showed it felt like a second inbox. That feedback cost a few hours. Hi-fi would have cost significantly more.

3
Navigation hierarchy is the thing most likely to break the whole thing.

Early iterations tucked the Directory (content preferences) in a hamburger menu. Testers missed it. That control needed to be a persistent tab, not hidden.

4
Trust is a design constraint, not a feature.

Because this app handles official academic communication, the entire visual language had to signal institutional trust. Color palette, typography, UI patterns — all rooted in LUMS systems. Students needed to believe this was official before giving it inbox access.

Clutterless outcome: A hi-fi prototype and full product specification, ready for stakeholder review. The research validates the need. The question left open: can an institution actually deploy it? That's a different problem from design.

01 — Why Build It

Teaching investing is easier with real stakes

Most university investing clubs teach with spreadsheets and historical data. It's passive. Students see a chart and forget it in a week. But when they're actually managing a portfolio — even a fake one — the emotional stakes are different. They read financial news. They debate analyst calls. They understand volatility when their own "money" is at risk.

The Pakistan Stock Exchange (PSX) doesn't have good third-party APIs for retail traders. There are real trading platforms, but they're either command-line tools (too technical) or slow web apps (too painful). No one had built something simple and fast for education.

02 — Product Definition

What's the minimum feature set for education?

I spent a week interviewing investing club members about what they needed. The clear answer: three things.

  • Real data, real speed: PSX prices update during market hours. Delays kill the learning.
  • Portfolio tracking: See your positions, P&L, portfolio composition. Visual dashboard.
  • Leaderboard: Competition drives engagement. Students check their rank more than they check their portfolio.

Everything else (advanced orders, margin, options) was noise. The MVP was: login → pick stocks → see your portfolio → check the leaderboard. That's it.

03 — Technical Decisions

Build fast, don't optimize prematurely

Data pipeline

PSX publishes prices every 5 minutes. I built a scraper that pulls the data, feeds it into PostgreSQL, and exposes it via REST API. No fancy caching strategy initially — just get the data accessible. Later added Redis for read-heavy endpoints (leaderboard queries).

Frontend: React + TailwindCSS

Needed to ship fast. React + Tailwind meant I could build a responsive dashboard in days, not weeks. Kept the component library simple. No design system at this stage — just consistency through constraint (one color palette, one type scale).

Auth: JWT tokens

Stateless authentication. Users login with email, get a token, use it for all API calls. Simple to scale. No session management overhead.

Deployment: Docker + AWS ECS

Containerized both frontend (Nginx) and backend (Node). Deployed to ECS for easy scaling. Used RDS for the database. Kept costs low by right-sizing instances — started tiny, scaled up as user count grew.

04 — Lessons Learned
1
Leaderboard gamification is more powerful than expected.

I almost didn't build the leaderboard because it felt like a "nice to have." But it became the primary reason users logged in daily. One trader said: "I check the leaderboard before I check my actual stocks." That's the PM lesson: understand what actually drives behavior.

2
Data freshness > visual polish.

Users care more about real-time prices than a beautiful design. Once I realized stale data was the #1 complaint, I re-prioritized. Moved Redis caching up the backlog. Design refinements waited.

3
Educational products need a story, not just features.

The thing that got users signing up: "This is how real trading works. You make calls. You see the outcome immediately." The platform didn't need to be complex—it needed to feel real. That narrative mattered more than feature count.

TradeUp outcome: A production platform with 100+ active users, processing real PSX data, and a fully productionized data pipeline. Proved that you don't need VC funding or a huge team to ship something people actually use.

01 — The Problem

Visual regressions take 2–3 days to debug manually

A typical QA workflow for visual bugs: developer pushes code → designer/QA reviews it on staging → spots a visual regression (button misaligned, color wrong, spacing off) → writes a bug report → developer investigates → fixes it → repeat.

Each cycle takes hours. Not because fixing is hard, but because the feedback loop is slow. The developer needs context about what broke, where it broke, and why. Bug reports are usually vague screenshots.

What if the system could do that work autonomously?

02 — Product Concept

An agent that fixes bugs before humans see them

The Sentinel does one thing: takes a screenshot or video of a visual regression, analyzes it with Gemini Vision, and generates a code fix.

The workflow:

  1. Designer or QA records a screenshot of the broken UI.
  2. Send it to Sentinel (via CLI or API).
  3. Agent analyzes with Gemini: "Button is 2px too small, text color is #333 instead of #000."
  4. Generates CSS/JSX changes based on the analysis.
  5. Opens a GitHub PR with the fix.
  6. Monitors CI/CD. If tests fail, rewrites the fix and pushes again.
  7. Once tests pass, the PR is ready for human review.

The goal isn't perfect automation (it won't be). The goal is to collapse the feedback loop from 2 days to 45 minutes.

03 — Technical Architecture

A self-correcting feedback loop

Input: Visual Analysis

Gemini Vision API ingests a screenshot or video. I prompt it with context about the UI (design system, spacing rules, color palette). The model returns a detailed analysis: "The error message is red but should be crimson. The padding is 8px but should be 16px."

Generation: Code Changes

Based on the analysis, the agent generates CSS and JSX changes. It has context from the GitHub codebase (pulled earlier in the pipeline). Changes are scoped — no refactoring, no random rewrites. Just fix what's visually wrong.

Execution: Playwright + GitHub

Opens a new branch. Applies the changes. Commits to GitHub. Opens a PR with a detailed message: "Fixed button alignment by adjusting padding from 8px to 16px. See analysis below."

Feedback Loop: CI/CD Monitoring

Waits for CI to run (tests, lint, build). If something fails, Gemini analyzes the error and rewrites the fix. If tests pass, the PR is ready for review.

This is not "AI writes all code." It's "AI handles the repetitive, well-defined part (analyzing visual diffs and proposing fixes). Humans handle the judgment (code review, final decision)."

04 — Why This Matters

AI-assisted debugging changes the PM's workflow

As a PM, you deal with visual feedback loops constantly. A designer says "this button looks off." A customer reports "the colors are wrong in dark mode." These are real problems that require fix time.

But they're also expensive because they interrupt the actual development flow. A developer has to context-switch from building a feature to debugging a color mismatch.

If an autonomous agent can handle that — analyze the visual problem, propose a fix, and even open a PR — then:

  • Developers stay in feature flow.
  • Turnaround time drops from days to hours.
  • QA can focus on edge cases, not obvious regressions.

The product implication: as a PM, you should be looking for these tools. Not "What if we use AI to replace QA?" but "What if AI handles the boring parts of QA so humans can handle the interesting parts?"

05 — Lessons
1
Vision models work surprisingly well for UI analysis.

I expected Gemini Vision to struggle with pixel-level accuracy. It didn't. With the right prompting (include design system context, specify the rules), it identified spacing, color, and alignment issues with 85%+ accuracy. Not perfect, but good enough to propose fixes that pass code review.

2
The self-correcting loop is the real innovation.

Version 1: Agent analyzes screenshot, proposes fix. 40% of fixes worked. Version 2: Agent monitors CI/CD, rewrites based on test failures. 85% of fixes worked. The difference: feedback. That's how you build AI systems that actually work at scale.

3
Automation isn't about replacing humans. It's about unblocking them.

The most powerful use case wasn't "fully autonomous QA." It was "QA can now focus on complex interactions instead of obvious regressions." That's a different product narrative. And it's more realistic.

The Sentinel outcome: A proof-of-concept that demonstrates AI-assisted debugging is viable. The bigger outcome: understanding that the future of AI tooling isn't replacement—it's working with humans to handle well-defined tasks faster.
